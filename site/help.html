Worried about yourself or a friend?

Reaching out isn't an overreaction, and it isn't a legal confession — it's the fastest way to get real support. These are confidential, non-judgmental places to start.

National toll-free drug de-addiction helpline

A government-run, confidential helpline for anyone seeking guidance or referral for substance use — commonly cited as 1800-11-0031 (Nasha Mukt Bharat Abhiyaan / Ministry of Social Justice & Empowerment).

NIMHANS Bangalore — Centre for Addiction Medicine

NIMHANS (National Institute of Mental Health and Neurosciences), based in Bangalore, runs dedicated de-addiction and psychiatric services and is one of India's leading centres for this kind of care. Look up current outpatient / helpline contact details on NIMHANS's own official channels before visiting.

Karnataka State de-addiction & counseling centres

Karnataka's Department of Health & Family Welfare and several NGOs run local de-addiction and counseling centres across Bangalore. If you're not sure where to start, the national helpline above can point you to the nearest verified local option.

How to start the conversation with a friend

  • Pick a calm, private moment — not right after you've found out, and not in front of others.
  • Lead with concern, not accusation. "I've noticed X and I'm worried about you" lands very differently from "You need to stop."
  • Listen more than you talk. People are far more likely to consider change when they don't feel cornered.
  • Offer to go together — to a counselor, a helpline call, or just for a walk to talk it through. Not having to do it alone lowers the barrier a lot.
  • Know your limits. You can care about someone without being their only support system — professional help exists for a reason.
