The Law

What Indian law actually says about weed

Legal status is one of the most misunderstood parts of the weed conversation in India. Here's the plain-language reality, in Bangalore's context.

⚠ General information — not legal advice
This page explains the law in plain language for awareness purposes. It is not a substitute for professional legal advice, and specific penalties depend on quantity and circumstances determined by courts. If you're facing a legal situation, consult a lawyer.

The core law: NDPS Act, 1985

Cannabis in India is regulated by the Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances Act, 1985 (NDPS Act). Under this law, "ganja" (the flowering/fruiting tops of the cannabis plant — what's commonly called weed) is a controlled substance. Its possession, cultivation, sale, transport, and consumption are criminal offences across India, including in Karnataka and Bangalore.

A common source of confusion: bhang

Some states treat bhang (made from the leaves of the cannabis plant, not the flower/resin) differently under state excise rules, which is part of why people assume cannabis is "basically legal" in India. This does not extend to ganja/weed (flower or resin) itself, which stays illegal under the central NDPS Act regardless of state.

How enforcement works in Bangalore

In Bangalore, NDPS Act offences are enforced by the Karnataka State Police (including city police and the Karnataka State Police's dedicated anti-narcotics units) and, for larger cases, the central Narcotics Control Bureau (NCB). Consequences under the NDPS Act generally scale with the quantity involved — the Act distinguishes between small, intermediate, and commercial quantities, with penalties ranging from fines to significant imprisonment for larger quantities. Because exact thresholds and sentencing details can change and are easy to misstate, we've deliberately kept this page qualitative — see the callout above.

What this means practically

  • Possessing, buying, growing, or sharing weed is a real legal risk in Bangalore — not just a health one.
  • A criminal record from an NDPS case can affect future education, visa, and job prospects — consequences that often outlast the immediate legal process.
  • "Everyone does it" doesn't change the legal exposure for the people who get caught.
Bottom line: weed is not a legal grey area in Bangalore — it's a controlled substance under a strict national law, enforced locally. If you want the exact text of the law, the NDPS Act, 1985 is a public government statute you can look up directly.
